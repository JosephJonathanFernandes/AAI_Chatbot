# **Literature Survey: Conversational AI and NLP Chatbot Systems**
## **1. Introduction to Conversational AI Evolution**
The field of conversational artificial intelligence has undergone a remarkable transformation over the past three decades. Early dialogue systems were predominantly rule-based, relying on hand-crafted grammars and pattern-matching heuristics to generate responses. These systems, while deterministic and interpretable, were brittle and incapable of generalising beyond their predefined rule sets. The emergence of statistical machine learning introduced probabilistic models that could learn patterns from data, yet their capacity remained constrained by feature engineering and limited contextual understanding.

The advent of deep learning, and subsequently the transformer architecture, fundamentally altered the trajectory of conversational AI research. Vaswani et al. (2017) introduced the self-attention mechanism as the core computational primitive for sequence modelling, eliminating the sequential bottleneck inherent in recurrent neural networks. This architectural innovation enabled parallelisable training over long token sequences and catalysed the development of large-scale pre-trained language models (PLMs). Subsequent work by Devlin et al. (2019) on BERT and Radford et al. (2018, 2019) on GPT demonstrated that contextual language representations learned from massive unlabelled corpora could be fine-tuned effectively across a wide range of downstream natural language processing (NLP) tasks.

Within the chatbot domain specifically, the taxonomy established by Hussain et al. (2019) distinguishes three principal categories: rule-based systems, retrieval-based systems, and generative systems. This classification remains widely cited and provides a useful framework for situating individual research contributions. Caldarini et al. (2022) extend this survey with a systematic review of post-2019 advances, noting that the dominant research directions include intent classification, dialogue state tracking, and the integration of external knowledge sources. Gao, Galley, and Li (2019, 2020) provide complementary neural-centric overviews, tracing the evolution from shallow retrieval heuristics through attentional sequence-to-sequence models to transformer-based generative systems.

## **2. Traditional Approaches: Rule-Based and Recurrent Architectures**
Prior to the transformer era, recurrent neural networks—particularly long short-term memory (LSTM) networks—constituted the dominant approach for sequence modelling tasks relevant to dialogue. Hakkani-Tur et al. (2016) demonstrated the viability of bidirectional RNN-LSTM models for multi-domain semantic frame parsing, achieving competitive performance on spoken language understanding benchmarks by processing utterances in both temporal directions simultaneously. This bidirectionality allowed the model to capture contextual dependencies without requiring explicit feature engineering for each slot type.

Liu and Lane (2016) advanced this line of research by proposing a joint model for intent classification and slot filling using attention-based bidirectional RNNs. The joint formulation allowed shared representations to benefit both sub-tasks simultaneously, improving generalisation compared to pipeline architectures where errors in intent detection propagate to downstream slot filling. However, these RNN-based approaches suffered from the vanishing gradient problem on extended dialogue contexts and struggled to scale to multi-domain settings without substantial task-specific engineering.

Rule-based chatbot architectures, surveyed in depth by Hussain et al. (2019), offered high precision within narrow domains but were fundamentally limited by the combinatorial explosion of hand-crafted rules required to handle linguistic variation. The maintenance burden of such systems rendered them impractical for open-domain dialogue, motivating the shift toward data-driven approaches. Bordes, Boureau, and Weston (2017) represented an early milestone in end-to-end learning for goal-oriented dialogue: their memory network architecture learned to respond in simple task settings (restaurant reservation, customer service) directly from conversational logs, bypassing the need for explicit state transition rules.

## **3. Modern Transformer-Based Approaches**
### **3.1 Foundational Architecture: Attention Is All You Need**
**Vaswani et al. (2017) — Attention Is All You Need.** The transformer architecture introduced multi-head self-attention as the sole mechanism for capturing dependencies across token positions, discarding recurrence and convolution entirely. By attending to all positions in a sequence simultaneously, the transformer enabled parallelisable computation and superior gradient flow over long sequences. The encoder-decoder structure established in this work became the canonical template for subsequent pre-trained models. Its impact on conversational AI is foundational: every major language model discussed in this survey—BERT, GPT, RoBERTa, LaMDA—is architecturally rooted in the transformer.

### **3.2 Pre-trained Language Models**
**Devlin et al. (2019) — BERT.** BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) introduced masked language modelling (MLM) and next sentence prediction (NSP) as pre-training objectives over a 3.3-billion-token corpus. Unlike autoregressive models, BERT conditions on both left and right contexts simultaneously, producing richer token representations. Fine-tuning BERT on downstream tasks—including intent classification and dialogue—demonstrated strong empirical performance across eleven NLP benchmarks. Crucially for chatbot research, BERT's contextual embeddings enable more nuanced slot filling and intent detection compared to static word vectors.

**Liu et al. (2019) — RoBERTa.** RoBERTa addressed several under-explored training choices in BERT: training for longer with larger batches, removing the NSP objective, training on more data (160 GB), and using dynamic rather than static masking. These modifications yielded consistent improvements over BERT on GLUE, SQuAD, and RACE benchmarks. RoBERTa demonstrated that BERT was substantially undertrained, implying that computational resources—not architectural design—were a principal bottleneck. This insight influenced subsequent dialogue research by motivating longer pre-training schedules for domain-specific chatbot corpora.

**Radford et al. (2018, 2019) — GPT and GPT-2.** The generative pre-training approach of GPT employed autoregressive language modelling on the BooksCorpus dataset, followed by task-specific fine-tuning with minimal architectural modification. GPT-2 scaled this paradigm to 1.5 billion parameters and demonstrated emergent zero-shot capabilities: without any task-specific training, the model generated coherent multi-paragraph text and answered reading comprehension questions at competitive levels. For open-domain chatbots, GPT-2 established that large autoregressive language models could serve as effective response generators, provided they were conditioned appropriately on dialogue history.

**Brown et al. (2020) — GPT-3 and Few-Shot Learning.** GPT-3 extended the scaling hypothesis to 175 billion parameters, demonstrating that in-context few-shot learning—where task examples are prepended as prompts—could match or exceed fine-tuned smaller models on numerous benchmarks. This work fundamentally redefined the interface between pre-trained models and downstream tasks: instead of gradient-based fine-tuning, dialogue systems could be conditioned through natural language instructions. The limitations of GPT-3 for deployed chatbots are significant, however: the model is computationally expensive, prone to factual hallucination, and lacks built-in safety mechanisms.

**Ouyang et al. (2022) — InstructGPT / RLHF.** InstructGPT addressed the alignment problem—the gap between language model fluency and human-preferred dialogue behaviour—through reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF). Human labellers ranked model outputs, training a reward model that was subsequently used to fine-tune GPT-3 via proximal policy optimisation. InstructGPT outputs were rated as more helpful, truthful, and harmless than GPT-3 outputs despite having significantly fewer parameters. This methodology underpins ChatGPT and represents the state of the art in aligning conversational AI with user intent.

**Thoppilan et al. (2022) — LaMDA.** LaMDA (Language Models for Dialog Applications) is a family of transformer-based dialogue models trained on 1.56 trillion tokens of public dialogue data and web text. Beyond raw pre-training, LaMDA undergoes fine-tuning for quality, safety, and groundedness—the latter involving the model generating search queries to verify its own responses against external knowledge. LaMDA demonstrated that specialised dialogue fine-tuning, rather than general-purpose language modelling, produces measurably superior conversational behaviour as rated by human evaluators across dimensions of sensibleness, specificity, and interestingness.

**Roller et al. (2021) — Recipes for Open-Domain Chatbots.** This work provided an empirical blueprint for building high-quality open-domain chatbots by combining retrieval augmentation, generative response generation, and knowledge grounding within a single blended framework. Human evaluations showed that engagingness, knowledge, and empathy are key dimensions on which chatbot quality is assessed. The paper identified a fundamental tension between model scale and specialisation: very large models excel at surface fluency but may lack the domain specificity required for coherent extended dialogue.

**Khatri et al. (2018) — Alexa Prize.** The Alexa Prize competition provided a large-scale testbed for open-domain socialbot systems, requiring sustained coherent conversation over multiple turns. Khatri et al. reported that combining multiple dialogue strategies—topic detection, sentiment analysis, question generation, and knowledge retrieval—yielded significantly better user ratings than single-strategy systems. The competition highlighted practical challenges including topic transitions, repetition avoidance, and graceful failure recovery that are not well-captured by standard academic benchmarks.

## **4. Intent Classification and Dialogue State Tracking**
### **4.1 Intent Classification**
**Chen, Zhuo, and Wang (2019) — BERT for Joint Intent Classification and Slot Filling.** This work directly adapted BERT to the joint NLU task by using the [CLS] token representation for intent classification and the token-level representations for slot tagging via a conditional random field (CRF) layer. Evaluated on the ATIS and SNIPS benchmarks, the BERT-based joint model outperformed prior BiLSTM-CRF and attention-based systems by substantial margins. The key insight is that the deep bidirectional pre-training equips BERT with slot-relevant contextual embeddings without task-specific feature engineering.

**Xia et al. (2018) — Capsule Networks for Joint Slot and Intent Detection.** Capsule networks, originally proposed for vision tasks, were adapted here to model hierarchical relationships between words, slots, and intent labels. Dynamic routing between capsule layers enables the model to learn part-whole relationships that flat classifiers cannot capture. On ATIS, capsule-based models demonstrated improved robustness for utterances with ambiguous intent signals, though at the cost of increased computational complexity compared to LSTM-based baselines.

**Zhang et al. (2020) — Few-Shot Intent Detection via Contrastive Pretraining.** Recognising that deployed chatbots frequently encounter new intent categories with minimal labelled examples, this work proposed a contrastive pre-training objective to learn intent-discriminative sentence representations. The approach achieves competitive performance under 5-shot and 10-shot evaluation conditions on CLINC150 and BANKING77, substantially outperforming fine-tuned BERT under the same data constraints. The limitation is that contrastive learning requires careful mining of hard negatives, and performance degrades under extreme label scarcity (1-shot).

**Casanueva et al. (2020) — Efficient Intent Detection with Dual Sentence Encoders (BANKING77).** This paper introduced the BANKING77 dataset comprising 13,083 customer service queries across 77 fine-grained intent categories. Using dual sentence encoders trained with a cosine similarity objective, the proposed USE-ConveRT model achieved high intent accuracy with significantly fewer parameters than BERT, making it practical for latency-sensitive chatbot deployments. The dataset itself has become a standard benchmark for evaluating intent detection in specialised domains.

**Larson et al. (2019) — CLINC150 Evaluation Dataset.** CLINC150 provides 23,700 in-scope utterances spanning 150 intent categories across 10 domains, augmented with out-of-scope (OOS) queries that no defined intent maps to. The inclusion of OOS examples reflects realistic deployment conditions where users frequently pose queries outside the system's capability envelope. Models evaluated on CLINC150 must therefore exhibit both high in-scope accuracy and robust OOS rejection, a dual requirement that exposes shortcomings of classifiers trained solely on closed-set intent distributions.

### **4.2 Dialogue State Tracking**
**Kim et al. (2019) — Dialogue State Tracking as Neural Reading Comprehension.** Dialogue state tracking (DST) is the task of maintaining a structured representation of user goals across conversation turns. Kim et al. reformulated DST as a machine reading comprehension problem, using BERT to extract slot values directly from the concatenation of dialogue history and a slot description string. This reading comprehension framing generalises naturally to unseen slot types and eliminates the need for fixed ontologies, addressing a major limitation of prior classification-based DST models.

**Huang et al. (2020) — Transferable Multi-Domain State Generator (TRADE).** TRADE employed a copy mechanism over dialogue context to generate slot values word-by-word, enabling the model to handle slot values that never appear in training data. The transferability across domains was achieved through shared parameters across all slot generators, allowing cross-domain knowledge transfer. On the MultiWOZ benchmark, TRADE achieved state-of-the-art joint goal accuracy while demonstrating that a single model could track state across five distinct service domains simultaneously.

**Wen et al. (2017) — End-to-End Trainable Task-Oriented Dialogue System.** Wen et al. integrated NLU, belief tracking, database query, and natural language generation into a single differentiable network trained end-to-end with supervised and reinforcement learning signals. This architecture demonstrated that task-oriented dialogue systems need not rely on modular pipelines with independently optimised components; instead, joint optimisation enables the system to discover internal representations appropriate for all sub-tasks. Key limitations include scalability to large domain ontologies and brittleness under domain shift.

## **5. Evaluation Methods and Datasets**
**Maroengsit et al. (2019) — Survey on Evaluation Methods for Chatbots.** This survey systematically categorised chatbot evaluation approaches into automatic metrics (BLEU, ROUGE, perplexity, F1) and human evaluation protocols (Likert scale ratings, comparative preference, task completion rate). The authors identified a critical limitation of automatic metrics: reference-based measures like BLEU correlate poorly with human judgements of response quality in open-domain settings because multiple valid responses exist for any given context. This observation motivated subsequent work on model-based evaluation metrics such as BERTScore and learned reward models.

**Caldarini et al. (2022) — Literature Survey of Recent Advances in Chatbots.** This survey examined evaluation practices across 60 chatbot papers published between 2019 and 2021, finding that human evaluation remains the gold standard but is expensive, non-reproducible, and subject to evaluator subjectivity. The authors recommended standardised evaluation rubrics and the adoption of publicly available test sets such as CLINC150 and BANKING77 to improve cross-paper comparability. The survey further noted that safety and fairness evaluations are underrepresented in the literature relative to their importance for deployed systems.

Dataset quality and annotation reliability are critical determinants of evaluation validity. Gururangan et al. (2018) demonstrated that annotation artefacts in NLI datasets—systematic lexical patterns introduced by annotators—allow simple classifiers to achieve high accuracy without genuine language understanding. Geva, Goldberg, and Berant (2019) extended this analysis to NLU datasets broadly, showing that models partially learn annotator-specific patterns rather than the underlying task. These findings have direct implications for chatbot intent classification benchmarks: high accuracy on ATIS or SNIPS may reflect dataset-specific biases rather than robust understanding. Swayamdipta et al. (2020) addressed this through Dataset Cartography, a training-dynamics-based method that identifies and removes annotation artefacts (easy-to-learn instances) to produce cleaner, harder evaluation sets.

Robustness evaluation represents an increasingly important dimension. Belinkov and Bisk (2018) showed that NMT models—structurally analogous to dialogue models—degrade severely under character-level noise such as keyboard typos and spelling errors, suggesting that chatbot NLU components require dedicated robustness testing. Sun, Li, and Qiu (2020) addressed a related challenge in multilingual and code-mixed dialogue (where users interleave languages within a single utterance), proposing adversarial training strategies that improved model robustness without sacrificing performance on clean input.

## **6. Summary of Research Gaps**
The surveyed literature reveals several recurrent limitations and open problems that motivate future research:

**1. Robustness to Noise and Domain Shift:** Despite strong benchmark performance, transformer-based chatbot models exhibit significant degradation under lexical noise (Belinkov and Bisk, 2018) and cross-domain transfer (Huang et al., 2020). Intent classifiers trained on clean transcribed speech fail on conversational text with abbreviations, misspellings, and code-mixing (Sun et al., 2020). Systematic robustness evaluation protocols are absent from most published work.

**2. Data Efficiency and Few-Shot Generalisation:** The majority of transformer-based methods require thousands of labelled examples per intent category to achieve competitive performance. Zhang et al. (2020) demonstrated that few-shot intent detection is feasible, but the gap between few-shot and full-data performance remains large. Methods that leverage unlabelled dialogue data and cross-lingual transfer are insufficiently explored.

**3. Dataset Annotation Quality:** Gururangan et al. (2018) and Geva et al. (2019) established that widely-used NLU benchmarks contain annotation artefacts that inflate reported accuracies. Intent classification datasets such as ATIS and SNIPS have not been systematically audited for annotator bias, raising questions about the validity of published results.

**4. Safety and Alignment:** While Ouyang et al. (2022) and Thoppilan et al. (2022) represent important steps toward aligning conversational AI with human values, safety evaluation remains non-standardised. Most academic chatbot papers do not report safety metrics, leaving deployed systems vulnerable to adversarial prompting and harmful content generation.

**5. Scalability vs. Practicality:** Brown et al. (2020) and Thoppilan et al. (2022) achieve high conversational quality through massive scale, but their computational requirements preclude deployment in resource-constrained environments. Casanueva et al. (2020) demonstrated that efficient dual-encoder models can match BERT accuracy for intent detection, yet equivalent efficiency results are absent for dialogue state tracking and response generation.

**6. Evaluation Metric Reliability:** As identified by Maroengsit et al. (2019), automatic metrics correlate poorly with human judgement for open-domain dialogue. No consensus metric exists for task-oriented dialogue either, with different papers reporting different subsets of metrics (inform rate, success rate, BLEU, entity F1), impeding direct comparison.

**7. Multi-Turn Coherence:** Bordes et al. (2017) and Wen et al. (2017) addressed task-oriented multi-turn dialogue, but their methods do not generalise to open-domain settings where no clear task structure constrains the conversation. Maintaining topic coherence, avoiding repetition, and tracking implicit user preferences over extended interactions remain open research challenges.

# **Concluding Remarks**
This literature survey has systematically reviewed 32 verified research contributions spanning the full arc of conversational AI development—from bidirectional RNN-based spoken language understanding to RLHF-aligned large language models. The central finding is that transformer-based pre-training has become the inescapable foundation for competitive performance across all dialogue sub-tasks, including intent classification, dialogue state tracking, and response generation. At the same time, the survey identifies critical research gaps in robustness, data efficiency, evaluation standardisation, and safety that represent fertile directions for the proposed NLP chatbot project. The datasets introduced by Larson et al. (2019) and Casanueva et al. (2020) provide well-scoped evaluation testbeds, while the methodological insights of Chen et al. (2019) and Huang et al. (2020) offer concrete technical baselines for the system under development.
